Illustrative Example: What's the Axis Point? Moving in a Circle

Example 2. A particle moving in a circle of radius R and angular speed ω.
A particle of mass m and speed v is moving in a circle contained in the xy - plane. Consider the point Q to be located at the center of the circle and the point S to be located a distance d below the center of the circle.

Find the direction and the magnitude of the angular momentum vector with respect to points Q and to S:

Answers: [show]
Question 1: What are the direction and the magnitude of the particle's angular momentum about point Q?
Check your answer: [show]
Direction: +z-axis, Magnitude: LQ = mRv = mR2ω
Solution: [show]

Consider the particle's position vector \(\vec{r}_Q\) measured from point Q as shown in the figure below.

Direction: Following the right hand rule, the direction of the angular momentum is along the +z -axis , parallel to the angular velocity.

Magnitude: The magnitude of the angular momentum vector is given by:

\[|\vec{L}_Q| = mr_Qv\sin\theta\]

where \(r_Q\) is the magnitude of the position vector and it is equal to the radius of the circle: \(r_Q=|\vec{r}_Q|=R\), and θ is the angle between the velocity and position vectors, which is 90o. The speed of the particle can be expressed in terms of the angular speed \(\omega\), as \(v=\omega R\).

Note:

  • The term mR2 in the expression of the angular momentum is the moment of inertia measured from an axis passing through point Q and perpendicular to the circle. In the figure, this is the z-axis: \(L_Q = mR^2\omega = I_z \omega\)
  • At any instant of time, the direction of the angular momentum vector about point Q is parallel to the angular velocity: \(\vec{L}_Q \parallel \vec{\omega}\)

The angular momentum vector about point S is perpendicular to the plane defined by the position and the velocity vectors; therefore it is at an angle \(90^{\circ} - \varphi\) with respect to the +z - axis, or at an angle \(\varphi\) with respect to the x-y plane.

Note: The direction of \(\vec{L}_S\) changes with time, whereas the direction of \(\vec{L}_Q\) does not, it is always parallel to the z-axis.

Note: If there were another particle of mass m circling the axis with the same speed as the original one but on the opposite side of the circle, its angular momentum about point S would have the same magnitude as the one of the original particle but in the direction of arrow 2. As a result, the angular momentum of each particles would have the same z-component and would have a component on the plane of the circle. These components would be opposite to each other and will cancel. The result would be that the angular momentum of the two combined would point along the z-axis. This is an example of a general law we'll learn later: the contribution to the total angular momentum due to the spin of a rigid body about a point in the axis of rotation is parallel to the axis of rotation and independent of the location of the point as long as the axis of rotation is passing through the center of mass of the object.

Question 3: What is the magnitude of the particle's angular momentum about point S ? (Express your answer in terms of ω, R, and the angle defined in the explanation of the previous question.)

Check your answer: [show]
\[L_S = \frac{mR^2\omega}{\sin\varphi}\]
Solution: [show]
Consider the particle's position vector \(\vec{r}_S\) measured from point S as shown in red in the figure below. By definition, the magnitude of the angular momentum vector with respect to point S is given by: \(L_S=mr_Sv\sin\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between vectors \(\vec{r}_S\) and \(\vec{v}\).

As shown in the figure below, the vectors \(\vec{r}_S\) and \(\vec{v}\) are perpendicular to each other: \(\theta=90^\circ\).

The position vector and its projection on the xy-plane form a triangle as shown in the figure below. Therefore the radius of the particle's circle can be written as \(R=r_s\sin\varphi\) and the magnitude of the position vector as: \(r_S = R/\sin\varphi\).

Note: We have derived that the magnitude of the angular momentum about point S is:
\(L_S = \frac{mR^2\omega}{\sin\varphi}\)
Multiplying by \(\sin\varphi\) the equation we obtain:

\(L_S\sin\varphi=mR^2\omega\)

The left hand side of the equation is the z-component of \(\vec{L}_S\):

\(L_{Sz}=L_S\sin\varphi = mR^2\omega\)

Recall that \(mR^2\) is the moment of inertia of the point particle about the axis through point Q. This is the z-axis in the figure. Then, the z-component of the angular momentum vector measured from point S is given by:

\(L_{Sz}=I_z\omega\)
