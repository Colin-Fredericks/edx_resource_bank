Illustrative Example: What's the Axis Point? Moving Along a Straight Line.

The angular momentum vector depends on the position vector of the point particle, therefore it depends on the point from which the particle's position is measured. This is illustrated in the following example where we find that the angular momentum has a different magnitude and direction depending on the point from which it is calculated.

Example 1. A particle moving along a straight line.

Question 1: What are the direction and the magnitude of the particle's angular momentum about point Q?
Check your answer: [show]
Direction: into the screen, Magnitude: LQ = mdv
Solution: [show]

Consider the particle's position vector \(\vec{r}_Q\) measured from point Q as shown in the figure below.

Direction: Following the right hand rule, the direction of the angular momentum is along the -z -axis (into the screen). This corresponds to a clockwise circulation about point Q as viewed from above the xy - plane.

Magnitude: At the instant shown in the figure, the magnitude of the angular momentum vector is given by:

\[|\vec{L}_Q| = mr_Qv\sin\theta\]

where θ is the angle between the velocity and position vectors. We will consider the supplementary angle β (θ + β = 180o) as shown in the figure:



Note:

  • The magnitude of the angular momentum depends on d, therefore it will have different values about different points in space. It depends on the specified point.
  • The distance d is the perpendicular distance: \( d = r_{\perp}\)
  • Because you know the perpendicular distance you do not need to know the angle θ or the magnitude of the position vector \(\vec{r}_Q\). The product of these two quantities is given: rQ sin θ = d. This is the moment arm method to find the magnitude of a cross product.
  • Also, θ and rQ depend on time but their product rQ sin θ = d is constant.
