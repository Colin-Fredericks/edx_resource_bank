Spin Angular Momentum of Planar Objects

We will now discuss the angular momentum of planar objects that rotate about a fixed axis perpendicular to the plane in which they are contained. As shown in the figures below, the two possible cases are: the axis of rotation passes through the center of mass of the object, or it does not (but it intersects the object at a given point - point Q in the right figure).

1. Rotation about an axis passing through the center of mass. 2. Rotation about an axis not passing through the center of mass.

Angular Momentum about point S:

\(\vec{L}_S = I_{cm} \vec{\omega} \)

where \(I_{cm}\) is the moment of inertia measured from the axis.

Note: point S is ANY point along the axis of rotation.

Angular Momentum about point Q:

\(\vec{L}_Q = I_Q \vec{\omega} \)

where \(I_{Q}\) is the moment of inertia measured from the axis.

Note: Point Q is the point of intersection of the axis and the plane.



1. Rotation about an axis passing through the center of mass:

When the axis of rotation passes through the center of mass and is perpendicular to the plane of rotation (the x-y plane in the figure), the angular momentum measured from a point S in the axis is given by:

\(\vec{L}_S=I_{cm} \vec{\omega}\)

where \(I_{cm}\) is the moment of inertia of the object about the axis passing through its center of mass. In addition, the expression above is the same for ANY point S along the axis. The angular momentum is parallel to the angular velocity vector and it is independent of the location of the point S, as long as S is on the axis of rotation.

Proof: [show]

Consider the same rigid object as the one in the previous page but now the axis of rotation is passing through the center of mass of the object.

As shown in the figure, the object consists of two small balls of masses m1 and m2, m2 > m1, connected by a rod of negligible mass. The object is placed on a horizontal and frictionless table contained in the x-y plane ans it is set to rotate counterclockwise as viewed from above the x-y plane with an angular speed ω. Therefore, the axis of rotation is perpendicular to the plane of rotation and is passing through the center of mass of the object. The distance between particle 1 and the center of mass is \(d_1\) and the distance between 2 and the center of mass is \(d_2\).

Consider now the point S located in the axis of rotation at a distance d below the center of mass.

We will show that the angular momentum about any point along the axis of rotation is parallel to the angular velocity vector and it is independent of the location of the point.

Before starting with the proof, note that the position of the center of mass measured from point S is given by:

\(\vec{R}_{cm,S}=(m_1\vec{r}_1+m_2\vec{r}_2)/(m_1+m_2)\)

where \(\vec{r}_1\) and \(\vec{r}_2\) are the position vectors of particles 1 and 2 measured from point S as shown below.

Writing the position vectors in terms of \(d_1\), \(d_2\) and \(d\), we have that at the instant when the rod is along the y-axis:

\(\vec{R}_{cm,S}=(m_1(-d_1\hat{y}+d\hat{z})+m_2(d_2\hat{y}+d\hat{z}))/(m_1+m_2)\)

rearranging terms,

\(\vec{R}_{cm,S}=((-m_1d_1+ m_2d_2)\hat{y}+(m_1+m_2)d\;\hat{z})/(m_1+m_2)\)    (eq. 1)

Because the point S is below the center of mass and both points, S and cm, are in the z-axis, then the position of the center of mass measured from point S does not have a component on the xy-plane and is given by:

\(\vec{R}_{cm,S}=d\;\hat{z}\)       (eq. 2)

Because (eq. 1) = (eq. 2), then

\(-m_1d_1+m_2d_2 = 0\)        (eq. 3)

We will use the relationship in (eq. 3) in our proof.

Now we calculate the angular momentum of the object as the sum of the angular momentum of the particles. (The rod does not contribute to the object's angular momentum because it has negligible mass).

\(\vec{L}_S=\vec{L}_S^1+\vec{L}_S^2\)

If \(\vec{v}_1\) and \(\vec{v}_2\) are the velocities of particles 1 and 2, then:

\(\vec{L}_S^1=m_1\vec{r}_1\times\vec{v}_1\)

\(\vec{L}_S^2=m_1\vec{r}_2\times\vec{v}_2\)

The angular momentum of each particle is perpendicular to the position and the velocity vectors:

\(\vec{L}_S^1 \perp \vec{r}_1\) and \(\vec{L}_S^1 \perp \vec{v}_1\)

\(\vec{L}_S^2 \perp \vec{r}_2\) and \(\vec{L}_S^2 \perp \vec{v}_2\)

As a result, the angular momentum of each particle about point S is not parallel to the angular velocity vector \(\vec{\omega}\) as shown below.

However, the total angular momentum is parallel to \(\vec{\omega}\). In order to prove this statement, we will add vectorially \(\vec{L}_S^1\) and \(\vec{L}_S^2\) by decomposing the angular momentum vectors along the xy-plane and along the z-axis.

For simplicity, we will do that at the instant when the rod is along the y-axis as shown in the left of the figure below:

The components along the xy-plane:

\(L_{S,xy}^1=m_1r_1v_1\cos\varphi_1\)       for particle 1

\(L_{S,xy}^2=- m_2r_2v_2\cos\varphi_2\)    for particle 2

where \(\varphi_1\) and \(\varphi_2\) are the angles between the angular momentum vectors and the plane, \(v_1=\omega d_1\) and \(v_2=\omega d_2\). Adding the components on the plane:

\(L_{S,xy}=L_{S,xy}^1+L_{S,xy}^2= \)

\(L_{S,xy}= m_1r_1\omega d_1\cos\varphi_1 -m_2r_2\omega d_2\cos\varphi_2\omega\)    (eq. 4)

From the figure, the cosines are given by:

\(\cos\varphi_1=d/r_1\)   and \(\cos\varphi_2=d/r_2\)

then \(\cos\varphi_1 r_1 = d\) and \(\cos\varphi_2 r_2 = d\). Replacing these relationships in (eq. 4) and taking \(d\) and \(\omega\) as common factors we obtain:

\(L_{S,xy}= (m_1d_1-m_2d_1)d\omega\)

Note that the term in the parenthesis is zero according to (eq. 3), therefore the component of the total angular momentum along the plane of rotation is zero and the total angular momentum is along the z-axis, parallel to \(\vec\omega\) as we wanted to prove:

\(\vec{L}_S=\vec{L}_S^1+\vec{L}_S^2 = (L_{S,z}^1+L_{S,z}^2)\hat{z}\)

We now calculate the value of the z--components:

\(L_{S,z}^1=m_1r_1v_1\sin\varphi_1\)       for particle 1

\(L_{S,z}^2=- m_2r_2v_2\sin\varphi_2\)    for particle 2

Adding the z-components:

\(L_{S,z}=L_{S,z}^1+L_{S,z}^2\)

\(L_{S,z} = m_1r_1v_1\sin\varphi_1 + m_2r_2v_2\sin\varphi_2\)    (eq. 5)

where the sines are given by \(\sin\varphi_1=d_1/r_1\)   and  \(\sin\varphi_2=d_2/r_2\)   then   \(\sin\varphi_1 r_1=d_1\)   and \(\sin\varphi_2 r_2=d_2\). Replacing these relationships in (eq. 5) we obtain:

\(L_{S,z} = m_1v_1d_1+m_2v_2d_2\)

Using that \(v_1=\omega d_1\) and \(v_2=\omega d_2\) in the above expression:

\(L_{S,z} = (m_1d_1^2+m_2d_2^2)\omega \)

The term inside the parenthesis is the moment of inertia of the object about the center of mass, \(I_{cm}\). Note that this expression is independent of \(d\), the location of the point S in the axis.

Summary:

We have shown, for this particular planar object, that for an axis perpendicular to the plane of rotation and passing through the center of mass, the angular momentum about any point S along the axis is parallel to \(\vec{\omega}\) and independent of the location of the point S:

\(\vec{L}_S = I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

This result can be generalized to any planar rigid object as long as it rotates about an axis perpendicular to the plane of rotation and passing through the object's center of mass. If the object is formed by a continuous mass distribution, then \(I_{cm}\) is not a sum but an integral.

Note: The moment of inertia of the objects that we use in this course can be found in a table. Therefore, there is not need to calculate the integrals (they have already been calculated for us). We can focus directly in the physics of the problems.



2. Rotation about an axis that does NOT pass through the center of mass:

When the axis of rotation does NOT pass through the center of mass but it is perpendicular to the plane of rotation and it intersects the object at a given point Q, then the angular momentum about the point Q is given by:

\( \vec{L}_{Q}=I_{Q}\vec{\omega} \)

where \(I_{Q}\) is the moment of inertia of the object about the axis of rotation passing through point Q. In this case, the angular momentum is parallel to the angular velocity vector only if it is calculated about the point Q. The angular momentum about a point not in the plane is not necessarily parallel to the axis of rotation! The details of this situation are beyond the scope of this course.

Examples of planar objects rotating about an axis NOT through the center of mass: [show]

In the examples shown in the following figures, the object is contained in the x-y plane and the axis of rotation is perpendicular to the x-y plane and parallel to ω. The axis of rotation intersects the object at point Q, which is at a distance d with respect to the center of mass of the object. Therefore, the moment of inertia about point Q can be obtained using the parallel axis theorem (or for the defining integral for the moment of inertia). If the mass of the object is M and the distance between the axis of rotation and the axis that passes through the center of mass is d, then:

\(I_{Q}= Md^2 + I_{cm}\),

where \(I_{cm}\) is the moment of inertia of the object about its center of mass.
