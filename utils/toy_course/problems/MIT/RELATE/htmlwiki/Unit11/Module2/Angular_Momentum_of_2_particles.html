We will start by calculating the angular momentum of an object consisting of two point masses and then we will generalize our result to the angular momentum of a rigid object whose mass is distributed continuously within the body.

Angular momentum of a system of point particles

The angular momentum of a system of particles is obtained by adding the angular momentum of each of the constituents.

Example: The Angular Momentum of Two Balls Attached by a Rod

Consider a rigid object consisting of two small balls of mass m1 and m2, m2 > m1, connected by a rod of negligible mass. The object is placed on a horizontal and frictionless table contained in the x-y plane. The object is set to rotate counterclockwise as viewed from above the x-y plane with an angular speed ω hence its axis of rotation is perpendicular to the plane of rotation and passing through the center of the rod, point Q in the figure.

Calculate the magnitude and the direction of angular momentum of the rigid object about point Q.

Answer: [show]

Magnitude: \(L_Q=(m_1R^2+m_2R^2)\omega\).    Direction: along the positive z-axis.

Solution: [show]

Consider the system to be the rigid object. The angular momentum of the system about point Q is the sum of the angular momentum of its constituents. There is no angular momentum associated to the rod because we consider it to be massless, then

\(\vec{L}_{Q} = \vec{L}_{Q}^{1} + \vec{L}_{Q}^{2}\)

Direction:

Consider the balls' position vectors measured from point Q to be \(\vec{r}_{1Q}\) and \(\vec{r}_{2Q}\), and the balls' velocity vectors to be \(\vec{v}_1\) and \(\vec{v}_2\) as shown in the figure:

Following the right hand rule, the direction of the angular momentum of each particle is along the +z axis and is shown in the figure below. Adding both vectors, the total angular momentum about point Q is also along the +z axis parallel to ω.

Magnitude:

The angular momentum of ball 1 with respect to point Q is:

\(\vec{L}_{Q}^{1} = m_1\vec{r}_{1Q}\times \vec{v}_{1} \)

and, the angular momentum of ball 2 with respect to point Q is:

\(\vec{L}_{Q}^{2} = m_2\vec{r}_{2Q} \times \vec{v}_{2} \)

where the position vectors of both balls measured from point Q have the same magnitude and equal to the radius of the circle:

\( |\vec{r}_{1Q}| = |\vec{r}_{2Q}| = R\)

Because the particles are connected by the rod they both have the same angular velocity of magnitude ω. As a result, their speeds are equal: v1 = v2 = Rω. The position and velocity vectors are perpendicular to each other, therefore the magnitude of the angular momentum vector of ball 1 is

\( L_{Q}^{1} = m_1|\vec{r}_{1Q}| |\vec{v}_1|\sin 90^o = m_1Rv_1 = m_1R^2\omega\)

and for ball 2:

\( L_{Q}^{2} = m_2|\vec{r}_{2Q} ||\vec{v}_2|\sin 90^o = m_2Rv_2 = m_2R^2\omega\)


The total angular momentum about point Q is:

\(L_Q=m_1R^2\omega+m_2R^2\omega = (m_1R^2+m_2R^2)\omega\)

The term inside the parenthesis is the moment of inertia of the rigid object about point Q, \(I_Q=m_1R^2+m_2R^2\).

Note:

In this particular example, the angular momentum of each ball about point Q is parallel to the angular velocity vector. Consequently, the angular momentum of the rigid object, which is the sum of the particles' angular momentum, is also parallel to the angular velocity:

\(\vec{L}_Q \parallel \vec{\omega}\)

Angular Momentum of a rigid object

If \(\vec{L}_Q^i\) is the angular momentum about a point Q of a particle 'i', then the angular momentum about point Q of a system of N particles is given by:  \(\vec{L}_Q=\sum_{i=1}^N\vec{L}_Q^i\). If the system is a rigid object then the sum over the particles will become an integral as shown in the following example.

Example: The Angular Momentum of a Hoop

Consider a hoop of mass M and radius R contained in the xy-plane as shown below. The mass is distributed uniformly throughout the hoop. The hoop is rotating with angular velocity ω about an axis perpendicular to the plane of rotation and passing through the center of mass, point Q in the figure.

Calculate the angular momentum of the hoop about point Q.

Answer: [show]

Magnitude: \(L_Q=MR^2\omega\).   Direction: along the +z axis.

Solution: [show]

We can think the hoop as being formed by a series of small elements of mass \(dm\). In the figure below a pair of elements of mass \(dm\) placed symmetrically with respect to the axis of rotation are shown.

Because we are assuming that the mass is uniformly distributed along the hoop, the two elements represent the point particles of the previous example with masses \(m_1=m_2=dm\). The angular momentum of each of the two elements dm are equal in magnitude and direction.

Following the steps of the previous example, the angular momentum about point Q of the element of mass \(dm\) in the right side of the hoop is equal to:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{dm}=dmR^2\omega\hat{z}\)

To obtain the angular momentum of the hoop we will add the angular momentum of each of the elements \(dm\) in the hoop. Because the mass is distributed continuously the sum becomes an integral:

\(L_{Q}=\int_0^M (dmR^2\omega ) = (\int_0^M dm)R^2 \omega = MR^2\omega = I_{Q}^{hoop}\omega \)

As we will see in the next page there will be no need to calculate any integral for the type of rigid objects that we will use in this course.
