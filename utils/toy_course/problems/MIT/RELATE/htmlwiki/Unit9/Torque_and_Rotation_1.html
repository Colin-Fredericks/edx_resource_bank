Angular Dynamics and Net Torque

In this unit we begin our discussion of rotational dynamics and rotational motion. We will start by introducing the concept of a rigid body, the new system constituent, and the concept of torque, the new manifestation of forces that produce a change in rotational motion.

Rigid Body

Up until now in this course, we have been treating objects as point particles. Point particles have no extent, and thus the only motion they are capable of is translation. Real objects, however, do have extent. They can do more than just translate — they can rotate or spin. In this unit we will introduce the next-simplest possible type of system: the rigid body.

A rigid body, unlike a point particle, does have a size and shape. It is a simplification of real solid objects: it has the property that the distance between any two points on the body remains constant – we will not be analyzing deformations like bending, twisting, or stretching. The property that they are rigid means that each and every part of the body rotates at a constant angular velocity, so that one variable — traditionally labeled \(\omega\) — can describe the rotational motion of the entire rigid body. An additional complexity is that a rigid body can rotate about any axis; hence \(\vec{\omega}\) is, in general, a vector. Fortunately, for the vast majority of this course, we will treat rigid bodies that rotate only about one axis — generally taken as the z-axis (with both the translational and rotational motion of the body being entirely in the x-y plane). Therefore \(\vec{\omega}\) will have only a z component, and we will often treat this component as a one dimensional variable. In this unit we will be even more restrictive: we will consider only rotational motion of a rigid body about an axis that is fixed in an inertial frame — i.e. it cannot change direction, and its translational motion is, at worst, motion with constant velocity.

Torque and Rotational Motion

This unit is focused on the Rotational Dynamics and Net Torque core model.

The concept of torque produced by a force represents a new way of describing the effect of interactions on the motion of an object. This concept will be defined in the next module.

We will be extending the idea from translational motion, namely

Interactions \(\rightarrow\) Force \(\rightarrow\) Change in translational motion

to include rotational motion. The net torque applied to a rigid body will produce a change in rotational motion:

Interactions \(\rightarrow\) Force \(\rightarrow\) Torque \(\rightarrow\) Change in rotational motion
