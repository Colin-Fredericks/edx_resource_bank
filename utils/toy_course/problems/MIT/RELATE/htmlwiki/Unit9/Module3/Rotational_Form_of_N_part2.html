Moment of Inertia

The Moment of inertia quantifies the inertia of an object for rotational motion and depends on how the mass is distributed with respect to the axis of rotation.

Moment of Inertia of a Point Particle

Consider a point particle of mass M moving in a circle of radius R, the moment of inertia of the point particle measured with respect to the center of the circle is given by \( I_Q= M R^2 \), where Q is the point at the center of the circle.

Moment of Inertia of a System of Point Particles

The moment of inertia of a system of more than one point particle can be obtained by adding the moment of inertia of the individual particles. The moment of inertia of N point particles rotating about an axis passing through point Q is given by:

\(I_Q=\sum_{i=1}^{i=N}m_ir_{\perp i}^2\)

where mi is the mass of particle i and \(r_{\perp i}\) is the "perpendicular distance" between the particle i and the axis passing through point Q.

Moment of inertia of an extended object

Finding the moment of inertia of an extended object requires an integral. To calculate it, we essentially consider the object as a collection of tiny masses. Then we figure out how far the masses are from the axis of rotation and perform a sum over the masses.

\( I= \text{sum of: lumps of mass} \times (\text{the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation})^2 \)

In the infinitesimal limit, the sum process over all the masses becomes an integration, and the expression for the moment of inertia becomes:

\( I_Q =\int r_{\bot}^{2}\; dm \)

Most of the time you do not need to perform the actual integration. You can find tables with the moment of inertia of the most common geometries in HyperPhysics or in Wikipedia . However, if you do need to evaluate the integral you should notice that:

  • \(dm\) represents the small lump of mass. In practicality, the mass of the small lump is its mass density \(\rho\) multiplied by the element of volume \(dV\): \(dm=\rho dV\). The element of volume can be expressed in cartesian, polar or spherical coordinates: \(dm=\rho dx dy dz\), \(dm=\rho r dr d\theta dz\) or \(dm=\rho r^2 sin(\theta) dr d\theta d\varphi\), depending on the geometry of the object.
  • \(r_{\bot}\) is the perpendicular distance of that \(dm\) from the axis, in terms of the coordinate system that you pick.
  • This is a definite integral: you are summing over the object, and the limits are usually the boundaries of the object.
  • Often the object can be considered in one or two dimensions (e.g. a thin rod or a thin disk), which will simplify the integral.
