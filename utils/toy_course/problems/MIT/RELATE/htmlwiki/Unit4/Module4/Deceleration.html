Acceleration vs. Deceleration

It is important to emphasize the specialized vocabulary of physics. So far, we have introduced two different aspects of motion. Each one can be discussed in terms of a vector concept (magnitude and direction) or in terms of a scalar concept (magnitude only). We discussed velocity, a vector, and speed, a scalar. If we are considering instantaneous velocity, then speed is the magnitude of velocity. The other quantity, acceleration, can also be discussed in terms of a vector acceleration or simply the magnitude, but for acceleration we have no special term for the magnitude. The vector is called "the acceleration" and the magnitude is "the magnitude of the acceleration". This requires the student to become sensitive to the word magnitude, which indicates an always positive absolute value. For example, the symbol "g" is the magnitude of the gravitational acceleration: g = +9.816 m/s2, but \(\vec g = -g \hat k\).

Everyday speech uses the physics vocabulary, but sometimes with less specificity, and occasionally with new words like deceleration. In everyday usage, speed is relatively faithful to its more precise physics counterparts in that it is used for scalar quantities without any sense of direction. The big problem comes with acceleration: in everyday vocabulary, this is often mixed with speed (as in a sports car advertisement). Moreover, whereas in physics acceleration is a vector, in everyday parlance it often connotes "acceleration in the direction of velocity" (when the velocity is increasing with time) and a new term "deceleration" is used when "the direction of the acceleration opposes the direction of the velocity" (when the velocity is decreasing with time). . Be careful to speak/think about motion precisely and realize that deceleration is not completely defined (e.g. if a negative velocity is decreasing, so acceleration is positive, is the object then decelerating or accelerating in everyday speech?)

Everyday Term Physics Equivalent
acceleration acceleration and velocity point in the same direction
deceleration acceleration and velocity point in opposite directions
