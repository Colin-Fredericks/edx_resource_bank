Simple Harmonic Motion of a Pendulum

Watching a pendulum should quickly convince you that the motion resembles SHM. In particular, there is a defined equilibrium point (hanging straight down) about which the motion is symmetric and periodic. Furthermore, the energy of the motion appears nearly constant (if friction and air resistance are small).

Let's try to solve for the motion of a pendulum and show that a pendulum undergoes SHM like a mass on a spring. The first complication is that the natural position variable for a pendulum is angle. Thus, the dynamics of the pendulum is governed by the Rotational Dynamics and Net Torque Model (with a fixed axis). Remember that the Dynamics and Net Force model (F = ma) led to SHM by first obtaining the differential equation

\[\frac{d^2 x}{dt^2} = -\omega_{\rm osc}^2 x\]

where \(\omega_{\rm osc}\) is a constant. This suggests that we should start with the equation

\[\tau_a = I_a \alpha\]

where the axis a is taken as the pivot as shown in this figure:

For a pendulum, the torque is provided by gravity. If we define \(\theta\) to be zero at the equilibrium position of the pendulum (straight down), then we have:

\[\tau_a = -mgL_{cm} \sin \theta_a\]

where \(L\) is the distance to the center of mass and \(m\) is the mass of the pendulum bob. Thus, the dynamical equation for the pendulum becomes:

\[-mgL_{cm} \sin\theta_a = I \alpha_a = I \frac{d^2 \theta_a}{dt^2}\]

This does not have the form of the SHM differential equation,

\[\frac{d^2\theta_a}{dt^2} = - \omega_{\rm osc}^2 \theta_a\]

the difference being that we have \(\sin\theta\) on the left hand side instead of simply \(\theta\).

Rather than wrestle with this equation, we restrict our attention to small angles of \(\theta\). It is well known that for small angles:

\[\sin \theta_a = \theta_a + \mathcal{O}(\theta_a^3)\]

Thus, if we restrict the motion of the pendulum so that:

\[\theta_a^3 < < \theta_a\]

where \(\theta\) is measured in radians, we can write:

\[-mgL_{cm}\theta_a \approx I_a \frac{d^2 \theta_a}{dt^2}\]

which can be rearranged to give:

\[\frac{d^2 \theta_a}{dt^2} \approx \frac{-mgL_{cm}}{I_a} \theta_a\]

which has the form of the Simple Harmonic Motion differential equation with

\[\omega_{\rm osc} = \sqrt{\frac{mgL_{cm}}{I_a}}\]

We have solved for a compound pendulum — one with a moment of inertia different from \(m L_{cm}^2\).

Simple Pendulum — Angular Frequency

If we have only a simple pendulum (a small, massive bob on a very light string of length \(L\)), the moment of inertia is approximately \(mL^2\) and the center of mass is at a distance \(L\) from the axis so we can simplify this to:

\[\omega_{\rm osc} = \sqrt{\frac{mgL}{mL^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}\]

One can see immediately that \(g/L\) has the dimensions of t-2, consistent with the dimensions of \(\omega\).

Angular Position vs. Time for Pendulum

After our small angle approximation, the differential equation involving \(\theta(t)\) is exactly of the form that implies SHM. We can thus write down the general solution for \(\theta(t)\) for a pendulum oscillating through a small angle:

\[\theta(t) = \theta_c \cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t) + \theta_s \sin(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

If a pendulum is released at a small angle \(\theta_0\) with a small angular velocity \(\omega_0\), its subsequent angular position will be (by analogy with the previous discussion of the solution for SHM):

\[\theta(t) = \theta_0 \cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t) + \omega_0 \omega_{\rm osc} \sin(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

Warning: there are two \(\omega\)'s in this expression: the oscillation frequency and the angular velocity. If you have not been careful to write \(\omega_{\rm osc}\) when discussing SHM, you will be at very high risk of mixing up the two \(\omega\)'s in situations where the angle of something is oscillating.

Ubiquity of Simple Harmonic "Motion"

As is implied by the case of the pendulum, any physical system in stable equilibrium can be set into harmonic oscillation, provided that the initial perturbation from equilibrium is small, where the exact meaning of "small" depends on the specifics of the system. Thus, a vast array of physical systems ranging from the deck of a suspension bridge to atoms in a solid lattice can exhibit SHM.

Additionally, the general form of the differential equation for SHM is fundamental to wave propagation. Thus, the transverse location of a portion of a string undergoes harmonic oscillation in a typical string wave, the air pressure undergoes harmonic oscillation in a sound wave, and so on. In this way, familiarity with the differential equation governing SHM has figured in many of the major advances of physics since Newton. In 1865 James Clerk Maxwell published a unified theory of electricity and magnetism that showed free electric and magnetic fields oscillate harmonically to create electromagnetic waves (e.g. microwaves or light). In 1916 Albert Einstein completed his general theory of relativity which predicts that the fabric of spacetime can oscillate harmonically to create gravity waves. In 1926 Erwin Schrodinger proposed a theory of quantum mechanics in which the quantum wave function that represents particles fluctuates harmonically.
