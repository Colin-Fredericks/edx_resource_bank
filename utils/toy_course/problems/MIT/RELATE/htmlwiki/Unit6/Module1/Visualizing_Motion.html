Visualizing Calculus of 1-dimensional Motion

General one-dimensional motion is calculus, pure and simple. In fact, Newton invented calculus to deal with motion. While the brain can easiily perceive velocity it has trouble with acceleration (except in special cases such as projectile motion). The best way to visualize acceleration is to connect graphs of position, velocity, and acceleration together in your mind using the rules of calculus.

Acceleration and Velocity

Acceleration is, by definition, the derivative of velocity. Derivative is the slope of the curve at a particular point, defined mathematically (and in physics) as the limit of the average velocity as the interval of the average decreases to zero.

\[ a(t) = lim_{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{v(t + \Delta t) - v(t)}{\Delta t} \]

If the acceleration is known, and you want to find the velocity, you have to do two things:

  • Start with a known velocity, \(v_x(t=0)\), and
  • Integrate the acceleration from \(t=0\) to the time \(t\) at which you want to find the velocity and add this to the velocity at t = 0 (often symbolized by v0).

    \[v(t) = v(t=0)+ \int_{0}^{t}a(t')dt' \]

Velocity and Position

The graphical relationship between the position and velocity is exactly the same as the one shown above between velocity and acceleration. The only new feature here is that the acceleration is the second derivative of the position vs. time. This enables you to connect the acceleration directly to the curvature of the position vs. time graph.

\[a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^{2}x}{dt^{2}} \]

Reversing this procedure yields a general expression for position that requires knowledge of the initial conditions. For example, the initial time is set to be t = 0 and the initial position and velocity \(x(t=0)\) and \(v(t=0)\) are given.

In order to obtain the position, start with the definition of acceleration, \(a(t)=\frac{dv}{dt}\) to obtain \(dv=a(t)dt\). Integrating this expression and using the given initial condition \(v(t=0)\), the velocity as a function of time is:

\[v(t) = v(t=0) + \int_{0}^{t}a( t') dt'\]

Using this expression in the definition of the velocity, \(v(t)=\frac{dx}{dt}\), and integrating \(dx=v(t)dt\) with the initial condition \(x(t=0)\):

\[x(t) = x(t=0) + \int_{0}^{t} v ( t=0)dt + \int_{0}^{t}\left(\int_{0}^{t} a ( t') dt' \right)dt\]

Because \(v(t=0)\) is a constant:

\[x(t) = x(t=0) + v(t=0) t + \int_{0}^{t}\left(\int_{0}^{t} a \left( t' \right) dt' \right)dt\]

The double integral will depend on the expression of the acceleration as a function of time.


Particular case. Constant net force - constant acceleration

If the total force acting on the system is constant then the acceleration is constant. After solving the first integral the above expression becomes:

\[x(t) = x(t=0) + v(t=0) t + \int_{0}^{t}a tdt\]

Integrating a second time:

\[x(t) = x(t=0) + v(t=0) t + \frac{1}{2} a t^{2}\]
