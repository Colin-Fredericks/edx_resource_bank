Independence of Translation and Rotation

When forces act on a rigid body, they may cause both translation and rotation. Their complete dynamics can be found by considering the motion to be made up of two parts:

  1. translation of the center of mass: \(\sum\vec{F}^{ext}=M\vec{a}_{cm}\)
  2. rotation about the center of mass: \(\sum\tau_{cm}^{ext}=I_{cm}\alpha\)

These equations apply independently of each other. The center of mass motion is independent of the point of application of the forces, and hence independent of the torque applied and of the resulting rotation. The rotational motion is independent of whether the center of mass is accelerating.

Example

To demonstrate this independence, let's look at an example.

The image below shows a rod with a force applied. You know that the rod will accelerate. Because the force is applied directly toward the object's center, there is no net torque therefore it will not rotate.

If we move the force off-center, the rod will rotate. However, it will also accelerate the exact same amount that it did before! Why?

In the unit of momentum we showed that the expression \(\sum\vec{F}^{ext}=M_{system}\vec{a}_{cm}\) determined the motion of the center of mass of a system constituted by point particles regardless of where the external forces are applied on the system and of how the point particles interact with each other. Thus, \(\sum\vec{F}^{ext}=M\vec{a}_{cm}\) applies to a rigid body regardless of where the external forces are applied.

It doesn't matter where we apply that force, the acceleration of the center of mass will be the same. The force is not "used up" to create torque. We get the torque "for free" with our force.

It may not be easy to keep applying a constant force in the same way on a rotating object, but if we can, we will get the same center of mass acceleration as we would on a non-rotating object.

We will also have additional kinetic energy due to the rotational motion. This results because, as the body rotates and translates, the force must act through a longer distance and hence will do more work.
