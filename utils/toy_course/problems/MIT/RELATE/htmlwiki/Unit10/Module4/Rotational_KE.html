Rotational Kinetic Energy

A particle in motion has kinetic energy. In the same way, a moving rigid object which is formed by an infinite amount of point particles, has a kinetic energy given by the sum of the kinetic energy of all its constituents. In this module we start by introducing the kinetic energy of a rigid object rotating about a fixed axis and continue with the kinetic energy of a rigid object translating and rotating.

Learning objectives

After completing this module you should be able to

  1. Define the kinetic energy of an object rotating about a fixed axis
  2. Define the Kinetic energy of an object that rotates and translates
  3. Apply the Mechanical Energy Model in a system with rigid objects in motion

Kinetic Energy: Rotation about a Fixed Axis

A rigid object rotating with angular speed \(\omega\) about a fixed axis passing through point \(Q\) is given by:

\(K=\frac{1}{2}I_Q\omega^2\)

where \(I_Q\) is the moment of inertia of the rigid object about the axis passing through point \(Q\).
Derivation: [show]

Consider the rigid object in the figure rotating with angular speed \(\omega\) about the axis passing through point \(Q\).

Each particle in the object is moving in a circle with angular speed \(\omega\).

For example, particle 1 is moving in a circle of radius \(r_1\), where \(r_1\) is the perpendicular distance between the particle and the axis of rotation. The speed of particle 1 is \(v_1=\omega r_1\), therefore the kinetic energy of particle 1 is:

\(K_1= \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2=\frac{1}{2}m_1(\omega r_1)^2=\frac{1}{2}m_1r_1^2\omega^2\).

Similarly, particle 2 is moving in a circle of radius \(r_2\) and its kinetic energy is \(\frac{1}{2}m_2r_2^2\omega^2\). Adding the kinetic energy of all the particles, the kinetic energy due to rotation is:

\(K= \frac{1}{2}m_1r_1^2\omega^2+ \frac{1}{2}m_2r_2^2\omega^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_3r_3^2\omega^2+ .....\)

\(K= \frac{1}{2}(m_1r_1^2+m_2r_2^2+m_3r_3^2+......)\omega^2\)

The term in the parenthesis is the moment of inertia of the rigid object measured with respect to the axis of rotation:
\(K = \frac{1}{2}I_Q\omega^2\)

Kinetic Energy: Translation Of and Rotation About Center of Mass

When a rigid object of mass \(M\) is translating with a center of mass velocity \(\vec{V}_{cm}\) and rotating with angular speed \(\omega\) about an axis passing through its center of mass, the kinetic energy of the object is given by:

\(K = \frac{1}{2}MV_{cm}^2+\frac{1}{2}I_{cm}\omega^2\)

where \(I_{cm}\) is the moment of inertia of the rigid object about the axis passing through its center of mass.

Note: The kinetic energy is the sum of two terms, the first term is the translational component of the kinetic energy, due to the fact that all the particles constituting the object are translating with the same velocity, \(\vec{V}_{cm}\). The second term is the rotational component of the kinetic energy. It represents the kinetic energy of all the particles rotating with angular speed \(\omega\) about an axis passing through the center of mass.

If the rigid object is not rotating, \(\omega=0\), then the expression becomes \(K=\frac{1}{2}MV_{cm}^2\).

If the rigid object is not translating, \(V_{cm}=0\), the expression becomes \(K = \frac{1}{2}I_{cm}\omega^2\).

Derivation: [show]

To derive the expression we will consider two frame of references. One fixed in space, whose origin is point \(S\) in the figure, and the other fixed at the center of mass of the object, point \(CM\).

Let us consider a point particle of mass \(m_i\) in the rigid object. The position vector of this particle measured from point \(S\) is \( \vec{R}_i\), and the particle's position vector measured from the \(CM\) is \(\vec{r}_i\). These two vectors are related as:

\(\vec{R}_i=\vec{R}_{cm}+ \vec{r}_i\)

where \(\vec{R}_{cm}\) is the position of the CM of the object measured from the fixed frame \(S\).

Taking the time derivative of the above expression we obtain the particle's velocity measured from point \(S\) is given by:

\(\vec{V}_i=\vec{V}_{cm}+\vec{v}_i\)

where \(\vec{V}_{cm}\) is the center of mass velocity and \(\vec{v}_i\) is the particle's velocity measured from the \(CM\).

To evaluate the kinetic energy of particle i we multiply by \(\frac{1}{2}m_i\) the square of the particle's speed:

\(K_i=\frac{1}{2}m_iV_i^2=\frac{1}{2}m_i\vec{V}_i^2=\frac{1}{2}m_i(\vec{V}_{CM}+\vec{v}_i)^2=\frac{1}{2}m_i V_{cm}^2+\frac{1}{2}m_i2\vec{V}_{cm}\cdot\vec{v}_i+\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2\)

Let us consider the rigid object to be formed by a set of \(N\) point particles. Adding the kinetic energy of all the particles:

\(K=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{N} K_i=\sum_{i=1}^{N} (\frac{1}{2}m_i V_{cm}^2+m_i\vec{V}_{cm}\cdot\vec{v}_i+\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2)=\)

\(K=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N(\frac{1}{2}m_iV_{cm}^2)+\sum_{i=1}^N(m_i\vec{V}_{cm}\cdot\vec{v}_i)+\sum_{i=1}^N(\frac{1}{2}m_iv_{i}^2)\)       (eq. 1)

Note that \(V_{cm}\) is independent of the index of the summation so it can come out of the summation. As a result, the first term in (eq. 1) can be expressed as: \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N(\frac{1}{2}m_iV_{cm}^2) = \frac{1}{2}(\sum_{i=1}^Nm_i)V_{cm}^2 =\frac{1}{2}MV_{cm}^2\), where \(M =\sum_{i=1}^Nm_i\) is the total mass of the object.

Because \(\vec{V}_{cm}\) is constant with respect to the index of summation, the second term in (eq. 1) can be expressed as \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N(m_i\vec{V}_{cm}\cdot\vec{v}_i)=\vec{V}_{cm}\cdot\sum_{i=1}^N(m_i\vec{v}_i)\)

Using the definition of velocity of the center of mass, the expression \(\displaystyle\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N(m_i\vec{v}_i)}{M}=0 \) because it represents the velocity of the center of mass measured with respect to the center of mass, then the second term in (eq. 1) is zero.

The third term in (eq. 1) represents the kinetic energy of the particles moving with respect to the \(CM\). An observer in the \(CM\) of the object sees the particle i moving in a circle of radius \(r_i\) with angular speed \(\omega\), therefore \(v_i=\omega r_i\), and the third term becomes:

\( \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}(\sum_{i=1}^Nm_ir_i^2)\omega^2=\frac{1}{2}I_{cm}\omega^2\)

Note: For a continuous rigid object \(N\) becomes infinity and the summation an integral.
