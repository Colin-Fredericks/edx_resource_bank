Describing Motion that Combines Rotation and Translation

This Unit addresses the general question: what happens to a rigid body that is free to translate and rotate when external forces act on it? We will also discuss the question of what happens when this motion is constrained in some way – for instance when a wheel rolls without slipping, or when a roll of tape is released while the tape is held fixed (imagine that it is a roll of Teflon tape that does not stick to itself).

Learning Goals

After completing this unit you should be able to:

  • Describe the dynamics of a rigid body as motion of the center of mass and rotation about the center of mass
  • Express constraints on translation of the center of mass and the rotation about the center of mass as a mathematical relationship between acceleration of the center of mass \(a_{cm}\) and the angular acceleration, \(\alpha\). For example, the constraint of rolling without slipping.

Translation Of and Rotation About Center of Mass

The central point of this chapter is that that the motion of a rigid body can be thought of as a combination of translation of the center of mass and rotation about the center of mass, each of which independently obeys the appropriate core model and associated equation of change. For a truly rigid body that cannot deform at all, the combination of translation of the center of mass plus spin about the center of mass are sufficient to reproduce any possible real motion.

Center of Mass Acceleration

In the Momentum and External Impulse core model we learned that

\(\sum\vec{F}^{ext} = M \vec{A}_{cm}\)

where \(M\) is the mass of the object (or system).

When this law is applied to a rigid body it determines the acceleration of the center of mass, and makes it clear that this acceleration does not depend on where on the body the forces are applied

Angular Acceleration of Translationally Accelerating Rigid Object

The law of change in the Angular Dynamics and Net Torque model, \(\sum{\tau}^{ext}_{axis} = I_{axis} \alpha\), applies only to fixed axis rotations — which requires that the axis is fixed in an inertial reference frame (i.e. is not accelerating). This is the same requirement as for the validity of \(\sum \vec{F} = m \vec{a}\), and it follows because the torque equation is derived from Newton's second law.

This raises a serious problem: if you apply a force on an extended rigid body that is free (i.e. is not constrained to rotate about a fixed axis), it will accelerate. Your intuition already understands that angular acceleration may well result – especially if the force is applied near the edge of the body. In the following example, shown below, the object will rotate about an axis passing through its center of mass. Because the object is accelerating the center of mass is accelerating as well, therefore the object is not an inertial frame of reference. The rod example motivates, but it is not a formal proof, the important case in which the torques determine the rotational motion of unconstrained rigid bodies: when the torques are taken about the center of mass. Therefore,

\(\sum \tau_{cm}^{ext} = I_{cm} \alpha\)

can be applied to any rigid body, regardless of how much the center of mass is accelerating.

Summary of Rotational and Translational Dynamics

When forces act on a rigid body, they may cause both translation and rotation. Their complete dynamics can be found by considering the motion to be made up of two parts:

  1. translation of the center of mass
  2. rotation about the center of mass

The corresponding equations of change are:

  1. \(\sum\vec{F}^{ext}=M\vec{a}_{cm}\)
  2. \(\sum\tau_{cm}^{ext}=I_{cm}\alpha\)

Remarks

Note 1: you will have to educate your intuition that these equations apply INDEPENDENTLY of each other.

  1. The amount of acceleration of the center of mass is independent of the point of application of the forces on the object.
  2. The amount of angular acceleration resulting from the forces is independent of how much acceleration of the center of mass they produce.

Note 2: The above discussion assumes that the rigid bodies are planar, or highly symmetrical, and that the applied forces are in the plane of the body or such that they do not tend to change the axis of rotation of the body. The motion of spinning rigid bodies in three dimensions involves advanced concepts of angular momentum that include the possibility that the momentary axis of rotation will not remain in a fixed direction even for a body with no forces acting on it!
