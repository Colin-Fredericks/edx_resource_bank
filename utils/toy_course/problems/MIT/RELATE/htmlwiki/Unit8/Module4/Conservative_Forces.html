Conservative Forces

Potential Energy

The potential energy is defined as the negative of the work done by a conservative force:

\(U(\vec{r}) = - \int_{\vec{r}_{ref}}^{\vec{r}}\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}\)

This expression may give you some intuition about the potential, which we extend by calculating the potential from the work integrals for several common conservative forces in the next two pages.

Conservative Force

A force is conservative if the work done by the force on a body that moves from an initial position to a final position is independent of the path taken.


To understand this concept we will consider the following situation:

A small box of mass m moves from point A on the horizontal surface to a final point B at the top of a building of height h. It can do so by following different paths but we will consider the two paths indicated in the figure:

Path 1: it moves along the horizontal surface, from point A until the bottom of the building, point c. Then it moves vertically upwards until it reaches point B.
Path 2: it moves from point A to point B up an incline of angle \(\theta\).

Find the work done by the force of gravity between points A and B for the two cases: when the box moves along path 1 and when it moves along path 2. [show]
Answer: [show]

The work done by gravity along path 1 and path 2 is the same and equal to \(-mgh\)

The work done by gravity is independent of the path
The work along path 1 - Calculation. [show]

We start with the definition of work:

\(W_{BA}^{path1}=\int_A^B\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}\)

Because the path changes at point c, the displacement vector also changes. As a result we write the work as:

\(W_{B,A}^{path1}=\int_A^c\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}+\int_c^B\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}\)

While the box is moving along the horizontal surface, the force of gravity is perpendicular to the displacement vector, therefore \(\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r} = 0\) and the work done by gravity between A and c is zero . When the box is moving up, the gravitational force is in opposite direction to the displacement vector. The work done by gravity between points c and B is negative, and the work along path 1 is calculated as:

\(W_{B,A}^{path1}=0+\int_0^h(-mg)\hat{j}\cdot dy\hat{j}=-mg\hat{j}\cdot\hat{j}\int_0^hdy=-mgh\)
The work along path 2 - Calculation. [show]

The box is moving along an incline of angle \(\theta\) with respect to the horizontal, therefore the gravitational force is at an angle of \(\pi/2+\theta\) with respect to the displacement vector. The dot product between these two vectors becomes:

\(\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}= mg\cos(\pi/2+\theta)dr = -mg\sin\theta dr\)

where we have used that \(\cos(\pi/2+\theta)=-\sin\theta\).

The work along path 2 is given by:

\(W_{B,A}^{path2}=\int_A^B\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}=-mgsin\theta\int_0^{d} dr\)

where \(d\) is the distance along the incline between the points A and B and is given by \(d = h/\sin\theta\). Then,

\(W_{B,A}^{path2} =-mg\sin\theta d = -mg\sin\theta h/\sin\theta=-mgh\)

Note: Another way to calculate the dot product is to write the components of the displacement vector in the x,y coordinate system:

\(\vec{dr}=dr(\cos\theta\hat{i}+\sin\theta\hat{j} )\)

where \(dr\) is the magnitude of the displacement vector, \(d\vec{r}\). This is the element of length along the incline. In the same way, we write the gravitational force in components:

\(\vec{F}_g=-mg\hat{j}\)

The dot product becomes:

\(\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}=-mg\hat{j}\cdot dr(\cos\theta\hat{i}+\sin\theta\hat{j})\)

After distributing terms and using that \(\hat{j}\cdot\hat{i}=0\) and \(\hat{j}\cdot\hat{j}=1\) :

\(\vec{F}_g\cdot d\vec{r}=-mg\sin\theta dr \)

The work along path 2 is:

\(W_{B,A}^{path2}=-mg\sin\theta\int_0^{d}dr=-mgh\)

Potential Energy Expressions:
In the example above we have shown that the work done by the gravitational force near the Earth's surface is independent of the path followed by the object. It depends only on the initial and final position (actually just the heights). The same result can be shown for the gravitational force far from the Earth's surface and also for the elastic force of a spring. In summary, potential energy gives us a way to account for the effects of a conservative interaction that does not require us to calculate the work from the corresponding interaction.

However, we will have to calculate the work done by a non-conservative force because its work depends on the path, therefore it depends on the particular situation present in each problem.
