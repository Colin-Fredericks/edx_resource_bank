Gravitational Potential Energy

Potential Energy of the Near Earth Gravity
\(U^{g}(y) = mgy\)

where \(y\) is the height of the object of mass m.

Potential Energy of Universal Gravity
\(U^g(r) = -G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r} \)

where \(r\) is the distance between the two masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) and \(G = 6.67 \times 10^-11 N \cdot m^2/kg^2\) is the gravitational constant.

Near Earth Gravity [show]

The work done by the near Earth gravitational force on an object of mass m was calculated in the previous module. Assuming a positive axis pointing up and gravity pointing down, then the work is given by: \(W_{f,i}=-mg(y_f-y_i)\) where \(y_f\), \(y_i\) are the final and initial height of the object. The change in potential energy of the object is obtained as:

\(U(y_f)=U(y_i)-W_{f,i}\)

or

\(U(y_f) = U(y_i) + mg(y_f - y_i)\;\)

Setting up a reference for the potential energy

Above we have defined the change of potential energy. We can also define the potential energy of an object if we set a reference value for the potential energy. In this case, we can set the potential energy to be zero at the surface, U(y = 0) = 0, then the potential energy becomes:

\(U(y)=mgy\)

Universal Gravity from Earth [show]
The work done by the gravitational force on an object of mass m far from the Earth's surface was obtained in the previous module:

\(W_{f,i} = G m_E m(\frac{1}{r_f}-\frac{1}{r_i})\)

where \(r_f\),\(r_i\) are the final and initial distance between the object and the center of the Earth. The change in potential energy of the object is:

\(U(r_f)=U(r_i)-W_{f,i}\)

or

\(U(r_f) = U(r_i) - Gm_Em(\frac{1}{r_f} - \frac{1}{r_i})\)


Note:

When an object is moving away from the Earth, \(r_f > r_i\) and \(W_{f,i} < 0\), so the kinetic energy decreases. By the same account, the potential energy increases. The result of the work is a change of kinetic energy into potential energy.

Conventional reference for zero of gravitational potential energy

Above we have defined the change of potential energy. If we set the initial position to be a reference point (like the origin of a coordinate system), then we can define the potential energy of an object relative to this point. In the case of far-Earth gravitation, we choose to set the potential energy to be zero at infinity: \(U(r_i)= 0\). If the final position is given by the radial distance \(r\), then the expression above reduces to:

\( U(r) = -G\frac{m_E m}{r}\)
